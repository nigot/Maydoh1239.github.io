Leaders of the Civil War

Abraham Lincoln

Abraham Lincoln was born on February 12, 1809, in Hodgenville, Kentucky. He grew up living near African Americans, both free and enslaved, which had a permanent impact on his views. This was evident, as he believed that if a slave was human, they were under the proclamation that all men were created equal, which was the view of most northerners. He was self taught, with little more than a year of formal schooling, but became a country lawyer and even won a case for a slave girl, Nancy, in 1839, letting her be freed in a historic court ruling. At age 52, he became inaugurated as the 16th president of the United States for the Republican party, which ultimately led to the Confederate Secession and the Civil War. On January 1, 1863, he issued the Emancipation Proclamation, declaring "that all persons held as slaves" in the rebellious states (confederacy) "are, and henceforward shall be free" (Abraham Lincoln, Emancipation Proclamation). After seeing the end of the Civil War, he was assassinated by John Wilkes Booth on April 14, 1865.

"Four score and seven years ago our fathers brought forth on this continent, a new nation, conceived in Liberty, and dedicated to the proposition that all men are created equal. Now we are engaged in a great civil war, testing whether that nation, or any nation so conceived and so dedicated, can long endure."
- Abraham Lincoln, Gettysburg Address, 1864

Robert E. Lee

Robert E. Lee was born on January 19, 1807, to revolutionary war hero Henry "Light-Horse Harry" Lee in Standford Hall, Virginia. Though financial hardship forced his father to move to the West Indies, Lee was able to get into United States Military Academy at West Point, and there he graduated second in 1829. In 1831, he married Mary Anna Randolph Custis, a descendant of John Parke Custis, who was George Washington's adopted son. After spending 17 years as an officer in the Corps of Engineers, he finally entered the battlefield during the war with Mexico in 1846. As part of General Winfield Scott's staff, Lee was quickly recognized, after earning three brevets for gallantry, and leaving the war as a colonel. He then served as superintendent of West Point from 1852 to 1855, teaching many of the men who would eventually fight for him and against him. He left West Point in 1855 to join the cavalry, and was involved in stopping John Brown's raid on Harper's Ferry. His military skill was recognized by Abraham Lincoln, who offered Lee command of the Federal forces in april of 1861. Lee declined and resigned from the army following Virginia's secession on April 17, arguing that he could not fight against his own people. Instead, he joined the Confederate Army as a military advisor to president Jefferson Davis, until he was given command of the wounded General Joseph E. Johnson's army on the Virginia Peninsula, which he named the Army of Northern Virginia. After stopping multiple attempts to take the Confederate Capital, Lee went on the offensive in September of 1862 with an invasion into Maryland to try to take the focus of the war off Virginia. Unfortunately, a misplaced dispatch outlining the invasion plan was discovered by Union commander George McClellan, removing the element of surprise, and the two armies fought in the Battle of Antietam. On September 17, 1862, the two armies fought to a stalemate, but following the bloodiest single day battle of the entire Civil War, with heavy casualties, Lee was forced to withdraw under cover of darkness. In June of 1863, after a Confederate victory at Chancellorsville, Lee felt inspired again to take the battle onto Union soil. The Confederate army met the Union army in the town of Gettysburg, Pennsylvania. For three days, Lee attacked Federal troops lead by General George E. Meade, with no avail. Finally, the battle reached its peak on July 3, 1863, in the Pickett's charge, where Confederates attacked the Union troops with a massive offensive aimed straight at the center of Meade's troops. After this failed, Lee knew the battle was lost and the Confederates retreated. Lee accepted full responsibility for the failure, and offered president Jefferson Davis his resignation, which Davis promptly refused to accept. After another Union victory at Vicksburg, Mississippi, General Ulysses S. Grant, took control of the Union forces, and changed the focus of his attacks from Richmond to simply destroying Lee's Army of Northern Virginia. Though the Union also took heavy casualties, the undersupplied rebels could not hold out, and Lee knew the end of the Confederacy was inevitable. The Confederates were forced into trench warfare by the summer of 1864, and though President Davis gave Lee full control of all remaining forces in February of 1865, Lee was forced to surrender his army to Grant at the Appomattox Court House, marking the end of the Civil War. Lee returned home on parole, and became the president of Washington College in Virginia till his death on October 12, 1870 in Lexington, Virginia.

"It is well that war is so terrible, otherwise we should grow too fond of it."
- Robert E. Lee
