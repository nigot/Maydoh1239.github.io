Leaders of the Civil War

Abraham Lincoln

Abraham Lincoln was born on February 12, 1809, in Hodgenville, Kentucky. He grew up living near African Americans, both free and enslaved, which had a permanent impact on his views. This was evident, as he believed that if a slave was human, they were under the proclamation that all men were created equal, which was the view of most northerners. He was self taught, with little more than a year of formal schooling, but became a country lawyer and even won a case for a slave girl, Nancy, in 1839, letting her be freed in a historic court ruling. At age 52, he became inaugurated as the 16th president of the United States for the Republican party, which ultimately led to the Confederate Secession and the Civil War. On January 1, 1863, he issued the Emancipation Proclamation, declaring "that all persons held as slaves" in the rebellious states (confederacy) "are, and henceforward shall be free" (Abraham Lincoln, Emancipation Proclamation). After seeing the end of the Civil War, he was assassinated by John Wilkes Booth on April 14, 1865.

"Four score and seven years ago our fathers brought forth on this continent, a new nation, conceived in Liberty, and dedicated to the proposition that all men are created equal. Now we are engaged in a great civil war, testing whether that nation, or any nation so conceived and so dedicated, can long endure."
- Abraham Lincoln, Gettysburg Address, 1864

Robert E. Lee

Robert E. Lee was born on January 19, 1807, to revolutionary war hero Henry "Light-Horse Harry" Lee in Standford Hall, Virginia. Though financial hardship forced his father to move to the West Indies, Lee was able to get into United States Military Academy at West Point, and there he graduated second in 1829. In 1831, he married Mary Anna Randolph Custis, a descendant of John Parke Custis, who was George Washington's adopted son. After spending 17 years as an officer in the Corps of Engineers, he finally entered the battlefield during the war with Mexico in 1846. As part of General Winfield Scott's staff, Lee was quickly recognized, after earning three brevets for gallantry, and leaving the war as a colonel.

"It is well that war is so terrible, otherwise we should grow too fond of it."
- Robert E. Lee
